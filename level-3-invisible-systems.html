Level 3 · Invisible when it works

The Invisible Systems

Have I built a real system behind the interaction? These systems keep "simple" simple at scale. If Level 3 works, the user never knows it exists.

1

Do I resolve conflicts dynamically, not statically?

Wherever elements compete for space, attention, or priority, am I resolving that with a live system that adapts to the actual content and context, or with hardcoded values that break on edge cases I didn't anticipate?

Try it, save a place, then zoom out with the slider
No collision system
Live collision detection
Zoomed outZoomed in

On the right, a small system continuously checks which labels would overlap and hides the less important ones. The purple saved label is a deliberate exception, it always survives, and you decide which place earns it (that's question 2 in action).

2

Do I have a hierarchy of what matters, with deliberate exceptions?

When there's too much to show, what's my ranking of relevance, and is it responsive to the user's context rather than fixed? And where does the ranking deserve a rule-breaking exception, chosen on purpose because it serves the user's actual goal?

Try it, pin the item you care about, then reduce the slots

Items drop in reverse priority order as space shrinks, except the one you pinned. You chose it, so it breaks the ranking on purpose.

3

Have I made the data model human instead of literal?

Systems that are technically correct can still be humanly wrong. Dates, boundaries, groupings, and labels should match how people experience their lives, not how the database stores them. Where does my literal model betray the human one?

4

Am I making users wait for things they don't need to wait for?

Assume success, update instantly, do the slow work in the background, and only interrupt when something actually fails. Every spinner is a decision. Is this one necessary, and do I have a graceful rollback path for the rare failure?

Try it, like both posts, then tick "simulate failure" and like again
Waits for the round trip

A spinner for something that succeeds 99.9% of the time. Every wait is a small tax on trust.

Optimistic update

Instant response, quiet background sync, and a graceful rollback on the rare failure.

5

Am I anticipating the user's next move, responsibly?

Preparing likely next steps in advance makes everything feel instant. But anticipation has costs in resources, privacy, and trust. For every speed trick, what is the user unknowingly paying, and is it worth it?

Try it, click the first button cold, then hover the second one for a second before clicking

Nothing loaded yet.

Nothing loaded yet.

Hovering signals intent, so the system starts fetching before the click. If you clicked without hovering, it loads normally. Nothing was wasted, nothing was assumed.

6

Can they ever lose their work, or their place?

Crashes, refreshes, dropped connections, and accidental backs happen mid-thought. Continuous save, restored drafts, and resumable flows mean the system absorbs the accident, the user never pays for it.

Try it, type in both boxes, then crash the app
Nothing saved

One crash and it's gone. The user pays for the missing autosave.

Continuously saved

Saved on every keystroke, restored after the crash, cursor and all.

7

Am I asking permission when I could offer undo?

Every "are you sure?" is the system taxing everyone because it can't reverse anything. Make actions reversible, delete instantly, and offer a short undo window. Reversibility is architecture, not a dialog.

8

Can two parts of the screen disagree?

If the header says one number and the card says another, trust dies in the gap. Bind every view of a fact to one source of truth so nothing on screen can contradict itself, even for a moment.

9

If everything works, will the user ever notice this system exists?

That's the goal. The compliment for Level 3 work is silence.

This space intentionally left empty. That's what great Level 3 work looks like.
Exit criteria

The interaction stays simple and instant under load, at scale, and at the edge cases, because a real system, not luck, is holding it up.
