Level 2 · Good vs. average

The Details

Have I handled the moments most people never notice? These details are invisible when they're right and infuriating when they're wrong. This level decides whether a product feels good or quietly frustrates people every day.

This level is mostly engineering decisions, not design decisions.
1

Is my feedback timed to when the user can act on it?

Validating too early punishes people for not being finished. Validating too late hides requirements they needed upfront. For every check, error, and confirmation, ask when the user is actually able to respond to it, and fire it then. Not before, not after.

2

Have I suppressed feedback where it would become noise?

Feedback that's helpful once becomes irritating at high frequency. Which of my confirmations, animations, or notifications fire so often that they should be deliberately turned off or toned down? Silence is sometimes the correct feedback.

3

Does placement match the user's next intent?

Don't position controls only for the current action. Position them for what the user will most likely do immediately after. If completing a common loop requires travel (scrolling, reaching, hunting), the friction compounds across every use, and at scale it changes user behavior.

4

Have I accounted for how this actually renders on real hardware?

GPUs and displays have physical quirks that don't show up on a dev machine. Colors, gradients, animations, and contrast can all degrade in the real world. Test on the devices your users actually own, in the modes they actually use.

Auto-corrections, sensible defaults, and tiny conveniences that take an hour to build. They quietly signal that someone cared. Their absence signals the opposite.

7

Do I accept input the way humans give it?

People paste phone numbers with spaces, type promo codes in lowercase, and leave a trailing space after their email. Be liberal in what you accept. Normalise before you validate, and never reject input a human considers obviously valid.

Normalises first

Trim, uppercase, then check. The parser forgives, so the person never knows there was anything to forgive.

8

Does focus go somewhere on purpose?

Not everyone drives with a mouse. Tab walks the keyboard through the controls, and whatever holds focus is where the next key press lands. Delete the focused thing and the browser silently drops the keyboard at the top of the page, unless you send it somewhere on purpose. Mouse users never notice, keyboard users feel every drop.

Exit criteria

Someone using the feature 50 times a day would have zero micro-annoyances, and couldn't tell you why it feels smooth, because nothing ever got in their way.
