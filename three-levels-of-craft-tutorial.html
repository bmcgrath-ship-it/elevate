The Three Levels of Craft

A hands-on companion to the team guide. Every principle below has a live demo, so instead of reading about bad UX, you get to feel it. Work through one level at a time, and try everything.

A self-review guide for engineers and designers

Ask these questions every time you ship. Each level is a tab with live demos, work through them in order, then finish with the checklist.

How to use this. Before you mark a feature "done," walk through the three levels. Level 1 is table stakes. Level 2 is where good products separate from average ones. Level 3 is where you build systems that make the product feel effortless. You won't hit Level 3 on every ticket, but you should always know which level you stopped at, and why.

Scope note. This is not a style guide, the design system owns how the product looks. These levels are about how it works, and looks only enter where they change what a person understands or trusts.
Level 1 of 3

The Surface

Everything visible on screen. Most failures here aren't code bugs, they're comprehension bugs. The user can see everything and still not understand it. Four principles, four demos.

Level 2 of 3

The Details

Invisible when they're right, infuriating when they're wrong. This level decides whether a product feels good or quietly frustrates people every day. Most of these are engineering decisions, not design decisions. Eight principles, eight demos.

Principle 2.1

Time feedback to when the user can act on it

Validating too early punishes people for not being finished. Validating too late hides requirements they needed upfront. Type your email into both fields and feel the difference.

Principle 2.7

Accept input the way humans give it

People paste phone numbers with spaces, type promo codes in lowercase, and leave trailing spaces everywhere. Normalize before you validate, and never reject input a human considers obviously valid. Enter the code with sloppy formatting in both fields.

Level 2 exit criteria. Someone using the feature 50 times a day would have zero micro-annoyances, and couldn't tell you why it feels smooth, because nothing ever got in their way.
Level 3 of 3

The Invisible Systems

Real systems that keep "simple" simple at scale. If this level works, the user never knows it exists. Nine principles, eight demos, and one deliberate silence.

Principle 3.1

Resolve conflicts dynamically, not statically

Wherever elements compete for space, a live system should adapt to actual content and context. Hardcoded values break on edge cases you didn't anticipate. Drag the zoom slider and watch both maps.

Try it, save a place, then zoom out with the slider
No collision system
Live collision detection
Zoomed outZoomed in

On the right, a small system continuously checks which labels would overlap and hides the less important ones. The purple saved label is a deliberate exception, it always survives, and you decide which place earns it (that's principle 3.2 in action).

TipFind your product's collision points, like long names, crowded lists, small screens, and translations that double string length. Then write logic that measures and adapts instead of constants that assume.
Principle 3.2

Rank by relevance, with deliberate exceptions

When there's too much to show, you need a ranking of what matters, plus rule-breaking exceptions chosen on purpose. Pin what you care about, then shrink the space and watch what survives.

Try it, pin the item you care about, then reduce the slots

Items drop in reverse priority order as space shrinks, except the one you pinned. You chose it, so it breaks the ranking on purpose.

TipWrite your ranking down as an explicit ordered list in the code, with a comment for every exception explaining whose goal it serves. Unexplained exceptions become bugs later.

Principle 3.4

Stop making users wait for the server

Assume success, update instantly, sync in the background, and only interrupt when something actually fails. Click both hearts, then try the failure case.

Principle 3.9

If it works, nobody will ever notice

This one has no demo, and that's the point. Every system on this level is invisible when it succeeds, from collision handling and ranking to human grouping, optimistic sync, and prefetching. The compliment for Level 3 work is silence.

This space intentionally left empty. That's what great Level 3 work looks like.
TipMeasure Level 3 by absence. Fewer support tickets, fewer rage-clicks, fewer "why is this slow" comments. If nobody mentions the feature, you probably nailed it.
Level 3 exit criteria. The interaction stays simple and instant under load, at scale, and at the edge cases, because a real system, not luck, is holding it up.
